Who actually owns Australian transport: the ownership breakdown

The ownership structure of Australian road transport is one of the most misunderstood things in the industry. Here is the reality.

72 of 97 operators are private companies. No public filings, no ASX announcements, no press releases about management changes. If you want to know who owns Linfox, you go to their website and it says “Fox family”. If you want to know who owns Centurion Transport, you need to check the ABR. If you want to know who owns MGM Bulk, you need to know the Giacci family sold Giacci Brothers in 2014 and started MGM as their bulk haulage vehicle.

9 operators are foreign subsidiaries of companies listed on overseas exchanges. Toll is Japan Post. DHL Express and DHL Supply Chain are Deutsche Post. Allied Express is Freightways NZ (NZX:FRW). Mainfreight is Mainfreight Ltd (NZX:MFT). ComfortDelGro Cabcharge is SGX-listed. When you are selling to these companies, the Australian operation may not have budget authority — the decision sits offshore.

5 operators are PE-backed, and this matters enormously for timing. PE-backed operators (FMH Group under Pacific Equity Partners, Team Global Express under Allegro Funds, Kinetic under its PE consortium, McColl’s under Friesian) are under active pressure to improve margins before an exit. They are buyers of efficiency technology, telematics, and process improvement — and they make decisions faster than family-owned privates.

7 operators are ASX-listed: K&S Corporation (KSC), Lindsay Australia (LAU), CTI Logistics (CLX), Qube Holdings (QUB), NRW Holdings (NWH), Harvey Norman (HVN captive logistics), and Aurizon (AZJ via Flinders Logistics). For these operators, their accounts receivable, capital expenditure, and fleet refresh plans are in the public domain.

Seven ownership changes in three years: M&A that changed the market

Between August 2022 and January 2025, seven significant ownership changes reshaped the Australian transport operator landscape. If you are working from a list that is more than 18 months old, you are selling to companies that may no longer exist under that name, or whose procurement structure has completely changed.

SRT Logistics → Lindsay Australia (May 2025, $108M). Tasmania’s largest refrigerated carrier is now a Lindsay subsidiary. If you had SRT as a separate account, that relationship now runs through Lindsay’s national procurement team in Cairns.

Silk Logistics (ASX:SLH) → DP World (2025, $175M). ASX:SLH delisted. A publicly listed Australian company is now a division of a Dubai-owned global port operator. Procurement approach has changed entirely.

Border Express → FMH Group / Pacific Equity Partners (March 2024). Border Express is now under the same PE umbrella as efm and CouriersPlease. Three separate accounts for anyone selling to all three are now potentially one conversation.

GJ Freight → Lindsay Australia (2024). WA cold chain absorbed into Lindsay’s national network.

Greyhound Australia → Entrada Travel Group (December 2023). No longer independently owned. Management and procurement changed post-acquisition.

Allied Express → Freightways NZ (August 2022, NZX:FRW). 1,100+ vehicle fleet. Australia’s largest courier network is now NZ-listed. Fleet refresh decisions involve Auckland.

BEVTRANS → SRV Road Freight (January 2025). Fleet absorbed. New Kenworth and Vawdrey equipment rolling out under BEVTRANS branding.

WA transport: seven operators that service Australia’s mining heartland

Western Australia produces the majority of Australia’s iron ore, lithium, gold, and mineral sands. Moving it requires a fleet of operators that the eastern seaboard has mostly never heard of.

Centurion Transport is the largest, running 2,000–4,999 vehicles with estimated revenue of $500M–$1B. Their network covers Perth, Karratha, Port Hedland, Newman, Broome, Kalgoorlie, Darwin, and Townsville — the full north-west mining corridor. Their clients include Woodside, BHP, Rio Tinto, and Wesfarmers.

CTI Logistics (ASX:CLX, $300M+) covers container transport from Fremantle nationally. BGC Transport is the captive haulage arm of BGC Group covering Perth metro construction materials. MGM Bulk (Giacci family, Bunbury) runs 130 prime movers and 400+ trailers hauling 16 million tonnes per year for Mineral Resources, Alcoa, and South32 — ultra-quad combinations at 210 tonne GVM. Cropline Group runs two divisions: Cropline Haulage (grain and fertiliser, Wheatbelt) and Bulkline Haulage (Pilbara mining bulk, primary contractor at Utah Point for Mineral Resources since 2016). Campbell Transport (Forrestdale, est 1997) runs 120+ quad and triple roadtrains covering 47,351km of road. Westina Corporation (trading as Jamieson Transport, Welshpool, 217 staff, $135M) covers general freight and mining logistics between Perth, Kalgoorlie, Esperance, Albany, and Geraldton.
